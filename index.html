Our Research

Introduction

Enhancing sports performance, particularly in basketball, can be achieved through advanced training and virtual technology. Athletes can improve their motion timing and skills, leading to better results in various sports. Utilizing virtual technology in basketball training not only rebuilds the training system but also deepens students' understanding, achieving a comprehensive development of skills. This study explores strategies for applying virtual technology in basketball, focusing on video-based motion capture systems like acoustic, mechanical, sensing, and optical systems. Although acoustic systems lack accuracy, mechanical and optical systems provide precise, high-speed motion tracking, though they limit movement and require proper markers. Monocular cameras, like Kinect, offer an affordable, integrated solution for capturing kinematic data efficiently. In Sri Lanka, basketball's popularity is rising, but traditional training methods relying on manual observation limit skill development and customization. Integrating computer vision, data analytics, and AI into training can overcome these challenges. This study aims to introduce a system that analyzes player and ball movements using smartphone camera feeds. The system will provide personalized feedback to enhance physical activity, shooting accuracy, and strategic thinking. This approach benefits both novice and professional players, helping them refine their techniques and enjoy the game more. Implementing such technologies can transform basketball coaching in Sri Lanka, making training more efficient and personalized, while tracking the development of young talent.

Abstraction

The scope and precision of basketball skill development are sometimes restricted using subjective evaluation and manual observation in traditional training methods. The goal of this research is to develop a complete platform that combines data analytics, machine learning, and computer vision to precisely deliver feedback on players' movements, shooting mechanics, strength training regimens, and player progress. The Computer Vision-Based Interactive Basketball Training Platform - Point Guard will use the existing literature on sports technology and methods of basketball training to present a game-changing solution for basketball training. The body will be tracked by computer vision algorithms to get quantitative feedback on a player's agility, footwork, and body alignment to refine all training paradigms. Hoop Tracking allows players to practice their shooting skills while their shot mechanics and trajectory are analyzed with state-of-the-art computer vision techniques. Strength Training is a set of individual exercise programs designed based on user feedback that emphasizes muscle-building training and training for injury prevention. Progress Tracking, in conjunction with Performance Analytics, consolidates all data from other components and feeds them into a sophisticated analytical framework used to measure individual growth, consequently giving output on a personalized training recommendation. In the end, the proposed system aims to enable members to enjoy the maximum potential of playing the sport, from the novice to the professional. The proposition is really to create a platform that amalgamates advanced technology with customization of skills and analytics for valid data that will focus on improving basketball performances

Research Gap

Following areas are the research gaps found in most of the recent researches.

Real-time Feedback.

The project offers real-time feedback via computer vision and hoop tracking but lacks focus on scalability, precision, and adaptability.

Performance Metrics.

The study tracks shooting accuracy and movement but lacks clarity on how these metrics impact long-term development and consistency.

Skill-Level Customization.

The system offers customized training but lacks insight into how it differentiates between novice and professional players over time.

Limitations in Training Data.

The models use specific datasets but don’t analyze their size, diversity, or how limited player data affects results.
